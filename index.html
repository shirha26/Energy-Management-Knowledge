Definition

A meter value is like a report card that an electric car charger sends to a central management system. Just like your electricity meter at home tells the power company how much electricity you've used.

When Sent

‚Ä¢ During charging (can be every few seconds to minutes)
‚Ä¢ When charging starts
‚Ä¢ When charging stops
‚Ä¢ On management system request

Purpose

‚Ä¢ Customer billing: Calculate exact energy consumed and costs
‚Ä¢ Safety monitoring: Ensure voltage, current, and temperature stay within safe limits
‚Ä¢ Energy management: Track total consumption across multiple chargers for grid optimization
‚Ä¢ Problem diagnostics: Detect equipment malfunctions through abnormal readings
‚Ä¢ Legal compliance: Meet regulatory requirements for energy measurement and reporting

‚ö° Energy.Active.Import.Register

‚ñº

Total Energy Consumption

What it is: This measures the total amount of electrical energy that has flowed into the car's battery.

Simple explanation: Think of this like the odometer in your car - it keeps track of the total distance you've driven. This measurand keeps track of the total energy that has been "driven" into your car's battery since charging started.

Real-world example: If you fill up a bucket with water using a hose, this would measure the total gallons of water that went into the bucket from when you started filling it.

Common Units:

Wh (Watt-hours) For measuring small amounts of energy
kWh (Kilowatt-hours) Most common - what you see on electricity bills
MWh (Megawatt-hours) For very large amounts (rarely used)

üîã SoC (State of Charge)

‚ñº

Battery Level Indicator

What it is: This shows how "full" the car's battery is, as a percentage.

Simple explanation: This is exactly like the battery indicator on your phone or laptop. When your phone shows 50% battery, that's its State of Charge.

Real-world example: If your car battery is like a gas tank, SoC tells you how full the tank is. 0% = completely empty, 100% = completely full, 50% = half full.

Common Units:

% (Percent) Always shown as percentage from 0% to 100%

üåä Current.Import

‚ñº

Electrical Flow Rate

What it is: This measures the electrical current (flow of electricity) going into the car's battery right now.

Simple explanation: Think of electricity like water flowing through a pipe. Current is how fast the water (electricity) is flowing. More current = faster charging, just like more water flow = faster filling of a bucket.

Real-world example: If electricity is like water, current is like measuring "gallons per minute" flowing through your garden hose. Higher current means electricity is flowing faster into the car.

Common Units:

A (Amperes or Amps) Standard unit for electrical current
mA (Milliamperes) For very small currents (1/1000 of an amp)

‚ö° Voltage

‚ñº

Electrical Pressure

What it is: This measures the electrical "pressure" that pushes the electricity into the car's battery.

Simple explanation: If electricity is like water, voltage is like water pressure. Higher pressure pushes water through pipes faster. Higher voltage pushes electricity through wires more effectively.

Real-world example: It's like the pressure in your home's water system. Low pressure = weak shower, high pressure = strong shower. Low voltage = slow charging, higher voltage = more efficient charging.

Common Units:

V (Volts) Standard unit for electrical pressure
kV (Kilovolts) For very high voltages (1000 volts)
mV (Millivolts) For very low voltages (1/1000 of a volt)

üî• Power.Active.Import

‚ñº

Energy Transfer Speed

What it is: This measures how much electrical power is flowing into the car's battery right now.

Simple explanation: Power is like the "speed" of energy transfer. It combines both the flow (current) and pressure (voltage) to tell you how quickly energy is being delivered to the car.

Real-world example: If charging a car is like filling a swimming pool with water, power tells you how fast the pool is filling up. It considers both how much water is flowing (current) and how much pressure is behind it (voltage).

Common Units:

W (Watts) Basic unit of power
kW (Kilowatts) Most common for car charging (1000 watts)
MW (Megawatts) For very high power (rarely used for individual cars)

Heat Monitoring

What it is: This measures how hot different parts of the charging station are.

Simple explanation: Just like checking if your phone is getting too hot while charging, the charging station monitors its own temperature to make sure it's not overheating.

Real-world example: It's like having a thermometer in different parts of the charger - the cable, the connector, internal electronics - to make sure nothing gets dangerously hot, like checking if your car engine is overheating.

Common Units:

¬∞C (Degrees Celsius) Most common worldwide (0¬∞C = freezing, 100¬∞C = boiling)
¬∞F (Degrees Fahrenheit) Used in United States (32¬∞F = freezing, 212¬∞F = boiling)
K (Kelvin) Scientific unit (rarely used in practical applications)
